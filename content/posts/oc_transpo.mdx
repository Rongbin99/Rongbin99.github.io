## Older Than You Think

For context, the oldest bus model still operational in the [TTC's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toronto_Transit_Commission) active bus fleet is the [2007 Orion VII NG (Next Gen) (Hybrid)](https://cptdb.ca/wiki/index.php/Toronto_Transit_Commission_1200-1423#Details). The [New Flyer Industries (NFI) D40i](https://cptdb.ca/wiki/index.php/Ottawa-Carleton_Regional_Transit_Commission_4203-4273#Details) buses in OC Transpo's fleet are from 2004. That makes these NFI D40i buses older than me and three years older than the Orions, which OC Transpo [retired in 2020](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/ottawa/ottawa-oc-transpo-bus-sale-1.5331534).

Ottawa, the Capital of Canada, is a heavily car-oriented city with subpar transit service and ridiculously high fares. You'll often see empty articulated or double-decker buses operating on weekends, only for them to suddenly disappear when a snowstorm hits because they get stuck in snow or struggle around roundabouts.

The [Transitway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitway_(Ottawa)) was a [Bus Rapid Transit (BRT)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bus_rapid_transit) network made up of bus-only rights-of-way. This essentially established a highway network exclusive to OC Transpo buses with stations, known as Transitway stops, serving commuters. With the first segment opening in 1983, a fraction of its prime still remains today.

OC Transpo operates a significant fleet of New Flyer D40i (Invero) buses built by [New Flyer Industries (NFI)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Flyer) between 2003 and 2006. Due to poor demand, the Invero lineup was discontinued in 2007, with OC Transpo being the main operator of them. With a distinct front hump that resembles a beluga, these buses introduced a top-mounted HVAC system that improved airflow circulation and later influenced New Flyer's next bus line, the [Xcelsior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Flyer_Xcelsior) ([XDE40](https://cptdb.ca/wiki/index.php/New_Flyer_Industries_XDE40), [XDE60](https://cptdb.ca/wiki/index.php/New_Flyer_Industries_XDE60), [XE40](https://cptdb.ca/wiki/index.php/New_Flyer_Industries_XE40), etc.).

Out of 326 buses originally ordered, only 29 are still actively in service as of April 2026 according to CPTDB (9 according to OC Transpo's [website](https://web.archive.org/web/20260417203925/https://www.octranspo.com/en/our-services/vehicles/), down from [55 in Jan 2026](https://web.archive.org/web/20260118143529/https://www.octranspo.com/en/our-services/vehicles/)). That's 8.9% (2.8%) of the original fleet that are still driving the roads of Ottawa, and they'll almost certainly be retired soon, marking the end of the Belugas. Since Jan 2026 alone, 13 units were retired (46 according to OC Transpo). The D40is are going extinct, so definitely enjoy them while you still can!

The [D60LF (Low Floor)](https://cptdb.ca/wiki/index.php/New_Flyer_Industries_D60LF) by New Flyer Industries was the first articulated bus of its kind to feature a low-floor design. OC Transpo ordered a substantial amount of them in 2001 and its successor, the [D60LFR (Low Floor Restyled)](https://cptdb.ca/wiki/index.php/New_Flyer_Industries_D60LFR), in 2010 to modernize and replace the aging fleet. Out of 280 units, 31 are still operating as of April 2026 according to CPTDB ([32 according to OC Transpo](https://web.archive.org/web/20260417203925/https://www.octranspo.com/en/our-services/vehicles/)) — 11.1%. The bulk of the D60LFs were replaced by the D60LFRs.

The D60LFR featured a curved exterior, hence the "restyled," plus specification upgrades. Everything else is mostly unchanged from the D60LF (minor patch update lol). Out of 306 units, 294 are still operating as of April 2026 according to CPTDB ([285 according to OC Transpo](https://web.archive.org/web/20260417203925/https://www.octranspo.com/en/our-services/vehicles/)) — 96.1% (93.1%).

Within the interior, there's a specific set of seats positioned in the middle at the "accordion" of the articulated bus. Due to the nature of articulated buses, the connecting flexible bellows (accordion) section goes through some aggressive bumps and bends, I call it the "rollercoaster" seat. Sitting here feels like you're riding a rollercoaster and about to fall off — yes, it can sometimes get you nauseous too.

Winter 2026 brought some unique challenges to OC Transpo with some brutal snowfall. OC Transpo frankly couldn't get the needed repairs done on its buses due to a [lack of qualified mechanics and a parts shortage](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/ottawa/oc-transpo-apologizes-after-surge-of-bus-cancellations-this-week-9.7040194) for its aging fleet (D40i, D60LF, D60LFR), creating a maintenance backlog and causing route cancellations. This forced many of these aging diesel models into retirement. If only OC Transpo hadn't [sold off its fleet of Orion VII NGs back in 2020](https://www.cbc.ca/news/canada/ottawa/ottawa-oc-transpo-bus-sale-1.5331534) due to reduced bus demand after the opening of the [Confederation Line O-Train LRT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_1_(O-Train)). To address this, OC Transpo has ordered a fleet of [New Flyer XD60s](https://cptdb.ca/wiki/index.php/New_Flyer_Industries_XD60), successors to the D60LFRs, to continue the presence of articulated buses in Ottawa.
